## Section 2: Study Purpose and Background

### 2.1 Research Question
How does a constitutionally-safe, adaptive AI learning system affect cognitive load, trust calibration, and user experience for neurodivergent learners compared to neurotypical learners?

### 2.2 Background and Significance
Constitutional AI offers promising pathways for safe AI systems, but existing work has not addressed adaptation to cognitive diversity. This pilot study tests a novel framework (Constitutional Adaptive Learning Theory, CALT) that integrates AI safety with neurodiversity-affirming design.

**Key Innovation:** Neurodivergent-first co-design with real-time safety monitoring.

### 2.3 Study Objectives
1. Test feasibility of Neuro-Adaptive Profile (NAP) instrument
2. Measure cognitive load variance across adaptive vs. static interfaces
3. Assess trust calibration (DTCI) in constitutional AI system
4. Evaluate usability (SUS) and sensory comfort
5. Validate instruments for cross-cultural megaproject

### 2.4 Expected Outcomes
- Preliminary effect sizes for power analysis
- Refined instruments (m-NAP, m-DTCI)
- Proof-of-concept for constitutional adaptation
- Community feedback for megaproject design

## Section 3: Study Design

### 3.1 Study Type
Mixed-methods pilot study with within-subjects design

### 3.2 Study Setting
Online, remote participation via web browser

### 3.3 Study Duration per Participant
- **Session 1:** 45-60 minutes (consent, NAP, learning tasks, surveys)
- **Session 2:** 15-20 minutes (delayed recall test, 2 weeks later)
- **Total time commitment:** ~75 minutes over 2 weeks

### 3.4 Study Procedures

**Phase 1: Consent and Baseline (10 minutes)**
1. Participant reads accessible consent form
2. Completes eligibility screening
3. Provides demographic information (optional)
4. Completes minimal Neuro-Adaptive Profile (m-NAP, 10 items)

**Phase 2: Learning Tasks (30 minutes)**
1. Participant completes 3 short learning modules on neutral topics (e.g., basic statistics, geography facts)
2. System randomly assigns adaptive vs. static interface for each module
3. After each module: cognitive load rating (Paas scale), trust rating
4. System logs interaction data (clicks, time, errors)

**Phase 3: Post-Task Surveys (10 minutes)**
1. System Usability Scale (SUS)
2. Sensory Comfort Scale (5 items)
3. Autonomy Index (3 items)
4. Open-ended feedback (optional)

**Phase 4: Delayed Recall (15 minutes, 2 weeks later)**
1. Email reminder sent
2. Participant completes recall test for learned material
3. Brief exit survey

### 3.5 Randomization
Within-subjects: Each participant experiences both adaptive and static interfaces in randomized order.

### 3.6 Blinding
Participants are not informed which interface is adaptive vs. static to avoid expectancy effects.

## Section 4: Participant Selection

### 4.1 Sample Size
N = 10 participants (with 20% oversample = 12 recruited)

**Justification:** Pilot study for feasibility testing and instrument validation. Effect sizes will inform megaproject power analysis.

### 4.2 Inclusion Criteria
- Age ≥18 years
- Fluent in English (or language of translated materials)
- Access to computer/tablet with internet
- Able to provide informed consent

### 4.3 Exclusion Criteria
- Age <18 years
- Unable to use web browser independently
- Severe visual impairment preventing screen use (unless using assistive technology)

### 4.4 Recruitment Strategy
- Social media posts in neurodivergent communities (Reddit, Discord, Facebook groups)
- University listservs and student organizations
- Snowball sampling through personal networks
- Flyers in disability resource centers (if permitted)

**Recruitment Materials:** See Appendix A (recruitment script)

### 4.5 Vulnerable Populations
This study includes neurodivergent individuals, who may be considered a vulnerable population. However:
- Participation is voluntary with no coercion
- Neurodivergent individuals are co-designers, not just subjects
- All materials designed for accessibility
- Participants can pause/stop anytime
- No deception or sensitive topics

## Section 5: Risks and Benefits

### 5.1 Potential Risks

**Minimal Risks:**
1. **Cognitive fatigue:** Learning tasks may cause mental tiredness
   - **Mitigation:** Built-in breaks, pause button, 30-minute time limit
   
2. **Sensory discomfort:** Screen time may cause eye strain or sensory overload
   - **Mitigation:** High-contrast mode, adjustable font size, optional audio, frequent breaks
   
3. **Privacy concerns:** Data collection may cause anxiety
   - **Mitigation:** Anonymous participation, no PII collected, clear data practices, opt-out anytime
   
4. **Frustration:** Difficult tasks may cause frustration
   - **Mitigation:** Tasks designed for moderate difficulty, skip option available, supportive feedback

**Risk Level:** MINIMAL — No greater than everyday activities like browsing the internet or taking an online quiz.

### 5.2 Potential Benefits

**Direct Benefits to Participants:**
- $50 compensation for time
- Opportunity to influence neurodivergent-first AI research
- Access to personalized learning profile (NAP results, if desired)
- Contribution to science

**Societal Benefits:**
- Advance understanding of constitutional AI for cognitive diversity
- Inform design of accessible AI systems
- Contribute to neurodivergent-led research movement

### 5.3 Risk-Benefit Ratio
Benefits outweigh minimal risks. Participants can withdraw anytime without penalty.

## Section 6: Informed Consent

### 6.1 Consent Process
1. Participant reads accessible consent form (8th-grade reading level)
2. Consent form available in multiple formats (text, audio, video with sign language)
3. Participant must check boxes confirming understanding of key points:
   - Purpose of study
   - Voluntary participation
   - Right to withdraw
   - Data handling
   - Compensation
4. Participant clicks "I consent" to proceed
5. Copy of consent form emailed to participant

### 6.2 Consent Form Elements
- Study purpose and procedures
- Time commitment
- Risks and benefits
- Compensation
- Confidentiality and data handling
- Voluntary participation and right to withdraw
- Contact information for questions
- IRB contact information

**See Appendix B for full consent form.**

### 6.3 Ongoing Consent
- Participants can withdraw at any time by closing browser
- "Stop and delete my data" button available throughout
- Reminder of voluntary participation before delayed recall test

### 6.4 Documentation of Consent
Electronic consent documented via timestamped log. No signatures required (online study).

## Section 7: Privacy and Confidentiality

### 7.1 Data Collection
**Collected Data:**
- Demographic information (age range, gender, neurodivergent status) — OPTIONAL
- m-NAP responses (10 items)
- Learning task performance (accuracy, time)
- Interaction logs (clicks, navigation)
- Survey responses (SUS, sensory comfort, autonomy)
- Open-ended feedback (optional)

**NOT Collected:**
- Names
- Email addresses (unless participant opts in for delayed recall reminder)
- IP addresses (anonymized)
- Precise location
- Any sensitive personal information

### 7.2 Data Storage
- Data stored on secure university server with encryption
- Access restricted to research team
- No cloud storage of identifiable data
- Backups encrypted

### 7.3 Data Retention
- Raw data retained for 3 years per university policy
- De-identified data may be shared publicly after study completion
- Participants can request data deletion within 30 days of participation

### 7.4 Data Sharing
- Aggregate results shared in publications and presentations
- De-identified dataset may be deposited in open repository (Zenodo, OSF)
- No individual-level data shared without explicit consent

### 7.5 Identifiers
- Participants assigned random ID (e.g., P001, P002)
- Email addresses (if provided for delayed recall) stored separately from data
- No linkage between email and responses after study completion

## Section 8: Compensation

### 8.1 Compensation Amount
$50 USD (or equivalent) for completing both sessions

### 8.2 Compensation Method
- Electronic gift card (Amazon, Target, or participant's choice)
- Sent within 1 week of completing Session 2
- Partial compensation ($25) if participant completes only Session 1

### 8.3 Tax Implications
Participants informed that compensation may be taxable income (consult tax advisor).

### 8.4 Compensation for Withdrawal
Participants who withdraw receive prorated compensation based on time spent.

## Section 9: Monitoring and Oversight

### 9.1 Continuous Human Audit Engine (CHAE)
- Weekly "audit buddy" sessions with neurodivergent volunteer
- Review system logs for safety concerns
- Public audit log on GitHub
- **Note:** CHAE supplements, does not replace, IRB oversight

### 9.2 Adverse Event Reporting
- Any adverse events (e.g., participant distress) reported to IRB within 24 hours
- Stopping rules: Study paused if >2 participants report significant distress

### 9.3 Data Safety Monitoring
- Weekly review of data quality and completeness
- Monthly review of recruitment and retention
